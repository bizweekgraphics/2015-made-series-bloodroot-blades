Meet the knife nerds in Arnoldsville, Georgia selling blades with a two-year wait list

By Author Name | July 23, 2015

Watch the video 3

When was the last time you were in your kitchen, cooking, and you looked at the knife in your hand and wondered, “Where did this come from?”

It was probably… never. Right?

But for David Van Wyk and Luke Snyder, the science-minded duo behind the knife company Bloodroot Blades, the source is half the point. They make gorgeous custom blades for a global clientele from their lakeside shop in Arnoldsville, Georgia, and for them, the first step in the process is finding their materials.

Unlike other knifemakers who start by grinding down stock pieces of metal, these highly sought-after blacksmiths go to junkyards and auto yards to find recyclable metals they can manipulate, whether it’s old springs from a 1954 Chevrolet, or discarded brass from a shipwreck. They transform these metals into stunning one-of-a-kind knives, so stunning that customers are willing to pre-order the blades over two years in advance.

“Everyone is different so we work to curate the right pieces of wood and pieces of metal,” Snyder explains. The wood might come from a clarinet factory in Paris, or a pecan tree in the workshop’s yard. It could be maple, cut down during a construction project in Atlanta and discarded. No matter what, no two knives by Bloodroot are exactly alike. “We're very committed to the fact that the right knife for one person is not necessarily the right knife for another,” says Van Wyk. To custom fit their knives, they’ll get on the phone, video chat, or have an in-person meeting—or several—as they figure out the details of each client’s grip and posture.

Since the metal they use is re-claimed, Van Wyk and Snyder must re-forge it themselves, using big propane burners that put out 500,000 BTUs and burn clean. From there, the metal is refined on a belt grinder and goes through many stages so that it will keep a keen edge and be durable. The last stage is a polish, after which it’s fitted for a handle. “The handling process is where we start thinking about the densities of the materials, the size of the person's hand, and where those balance points need to be,” says Snyder.

The very last things the masterful knife-makers do is to put the edge on the blade. “We work the edge itself down to this amazing geometry, which itself is a kind of art,” Van Wyk explains. “You'll see the polished edge and the width of the edge will tell you a lot about a knife, like how well it's made. This is where the rubber meets the road.”

The most exciting part of the process arguably happens after the knives leave Bloodroot’s shop, when people start to actually put them to work—in the field to dress animals, in kitchens to filet fish or chop onions, or at the table to cut up steaks. That’s when the knives really come into their own. “It's like wearing a pair of blue jeans to where it starts to feel good,” explains Van Wyk. He’s talking about the patina that develops on the high-carbon steel, about the slow, gradual graying along the metal. The more you use a knife like this, the more it belongs in your hands.
